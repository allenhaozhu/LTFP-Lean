## Statement

> **Proposition 1.3 (McDiarmid's inequality)** Let $Z_1, \dots, Z_n$ be independent
> random variables (in any measurable space $\mathcal{Z}$), and $f : \mathcal{Z}^n \to \mathbb{R}$
> a function of "bounded variation"; that is, such that for all $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$,
> and all $z_1, \dots, z_n, z_i' \in \mathcal{Z}$, we have
> $$\bigl| f(z_1, \dots, z_{i-1}, z_i, z_{i+1}, \dots, z_n) - f(z_1, \dots, z_{i-1}, z_i', z_{i+1}, \dots, z_n) \bigr| \;\le\; c.$$
> Then
> $$\mathbb{P}\!\bigl( |f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) - \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n)]| \ge t \bigr) \;\le\; 2 \exp\!\bigl(-2 t^2 / (n c^2)\bigr).$$

## Proof (verbatim)

> **Proof (♦).** The proof generalizes the formulation of Hoeffding's inequality in
> equation (1.8), which corresponds to $f(z) = \tfrac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} z_i$ and
> $c = \tfrac{1}{n}$. We will only consider the one-sided inequality:
> $$\mathbb{P}\!\bigl( f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) - \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n)] \ge t \bigr) \;\le\; \exp(-2t^2/(nc^2)),$$
> which is sufficient to get the two-sided inequality using the union bound.
>
> We introduce the random variables, for $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$:
> $$V_i = \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_i] - \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_{i-1}],$$
> with $V_1 = \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) \mid Z_1] - \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n)]$.
> We have $\mathbb{E}[V_i \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_{i-1}] = 0$. Moreover, given
> $Z_1, \dots, Z_{i-1}$, the maximal value of $V_i$ minus the minimal value of $V_i$
> is almost surely less than $c$ as a consequence of the bounded variation assumption,
> since it is the difference of two terms that are conditional expectations of values
> of $f$ taken at arguments that only differ in the $i$th variable. Moreover, through
> a telescoping sum, we have $f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n) - \mathbb{E}[f(Z_1, \dots, Z_n)] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} V_i$.
> Using the same argument as in part (1) of the proof of Hoeffding's inequality (page 10),
> we get for any $s > 0$, $\mathbb{E}[e^{s V_i} \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_{i-1}] \le e^{s^2 c^2/8}$,
> and we can obtain a proof with the same steps as part (2) of the same proof (page 10)
> by being careful with conditioning, for any $s > 0$:
> $$\mathbb{P}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n V_i \ge t\right) \;\le\; \exp(-st) \cdot \mathbb{E}\exp\!\left(s\sum_{i=1}^n V_i\right) \text{ using Markov's inequality,}$$
> $$= \exp(-st) \cdot \mathbb{E}\!\left[\exp\!\left(s\sum_{i=1}^{n-1} V_i\right) \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\exp(s V_n) \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_{n-1}\bigr]\right],$$
> since $V_1, \dots, V_{n-1}$ are in the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $Z_1, \dots, Z_{n-1}$,
> $$\le \exp(-st + s^2 c^2/8) \cdot \mathbb{E}\exp\!\left(s\sum_{i=1}^{n-1} V_i\right),$$
> using the bound on $\mathbb{E}[e^{sV_n} \mid Z_1, \dots, Z_{n-1}]$ given above. Applying
> the same reasoning $n$ times, we get a probability that is less than
> $\exp(-st + ns^2 c^2/8)$ and the desired result by minimizing with respect to $s$
> (leading to $s = 4t/(nc^2)$).

## Notes

- Key intermediate lemma reused from §1.2.1: the conditional MGF bound
  $\mathbb{E}[e^{sV_i} \mid Z_1,\dots,Z_{i-1}] \le e^{s^2 c^2/8}$, which is Hoeffding's
  lemma applied to the bounded martingale increment $V_i$ of range $\le c$.
- Bach's proof technique: write $f - \mathbb{E}f$ as a Doob martingale sum $\sum_i V_i$,
  apply the Hoeffding MGF bound conditionally inside an iterated tower of expectations,
  then exponentiate and optimize $s$ as in the i.i.d. proof.
- Bach marks the proof with ♦ (diamond difficulty: "harder, can be skipped on first read").
- The constant $c$ here is the per-coordinate variation bound; for the i.i.d. average
  $f(z) = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum z_i$ this is $c = 1/n$, recovering equation (1.8).
- Bach references this inequality (page 14) as the tool used to provide high-probability
  bounds on the estimation error in empirical risk minimization in §4.4.1.
